Harith did not follow her.

That was the first thing Aleesya remembered the next morning.

Not his confession. Not the way his voice had broken around the word want. Not the reservoir lights trembling across black water, or the pastry bag crushed in his hand, or the look on his face when she said, “I did love you.”

She remembered that he stayed where he was.

He did not call her name again. He did not walk behind her in the rain-damp evening. He did not send a message before she reached the bus stop, did not ask whether she was safe, did not try to turn his pain into an emergency she had to manage. For once, Harith had let her leave without making her responsible for how leaving hurt him.

That should not have felt like care.

But it did.

Sunday morning came soft and grey, with the kind of light that made the whole flat seem quieter than usual. Aleesya woke before her alarm and lay still beneath her blanket, listening to the old sounds of home. Her mother moving in the kitchen. The scrape of a chair. The low murmur of Qur’an from her father’s phone in the living room. Rainwater dripping somewhere outside long after the rain itself had stopped.

Her phone was on the desk, far enough that she would have to get out of bed to reach it.

She had placed it there deliberately.

The old Aleesya would have slept with it beside her pillow after a night like that, face turned toward the screen, body tuned to every possible buzz. The old Aleesya would have rehearsed replies before receiving messages, imagined apologies, imagined silence, imagined Harith standing at the edge of some decision and needing her gentleness to cross it.

The old Aleesya had been very good at waiting.

The new one was still learning what to do with her hands.

She got out of bed, made it, then stood by the desk for a long moment before unlocking her phone.

No message from Harith.

Her chest tightened.

Then loosened.

The contradiction was becoming familiar.

There was one message from Danish, sent the night before at 10:16 p.m.

> Danish
> Hope you reached home safely. No need to reply tonight. Thank you again for today.

Aleesya looked at the timestamp. He had sent it after their coffee, before she met Harith at the reservoir. A simple message from a man who had no idea he had walked into the middle of an unfinished story and still managed not to step on anything carelessly.

She typed:

> Aleesya
> I reached home safely. Thank you for coffee yesterday.

Then she paused.

The honest thing sat behind her teeth, waiting.

Danish had been clear with her. She did not owe him the whole wound, but she owed him enough truth not to make him a shelter she hid inside without warning him of the storm.

She added:

> Aleesya
> I should also be honest. There’s something from before that I’m still sorting through. I don’t want to be unfair to you.

She sent it before she could edit it into something safer.

His reply came ten minutes later, while she was brushing her teeth.

> Danish
> Thank you for telling me. I appreciate the honesty. Take your time. Coffee was still coffee, not a contract.

Aleesya laughed softly into the sink, toothpaste on her lips, eyes unexpectedly wet.

Coffee was still coffee, not a contract.

How strange, she thought, that being treated gently could make a person grieve all the times she had accepted less.

Harith did not sleep much after the reservoir.

He went home with the pastry box crushed at one corner and his mother’s irritation waiting at the door. She scolded him for not answering her call, then stopped halfway when she saw his face. That was the problem with mothers. They could be angry and worried in the same breath, and sometimes the worry won before the anger had finished its sentence.

“Kenapa?” she asked, softer now. What happened?

Harith removed his shoes slowly. “Nothing.”

His mother gave him a look that had survived raising three children and several decades of his father pretending not to have high blood pressure. “Don’t nothing me. Your face looks like someone passed away.”

He almost said, maybe something did.

Instead, he held out the pastry bag. “Sorry. It got wet.”

His mother took it, her expression shifting from suspicion to concern. “Harith.”

He could not look at her. “I’m tired, Ibu.”

For once, she let him go.

In his room, Harith sat on the edge of his bed in the dark without switching on the light. His shirt still smelled faintly of rain and the bakery. His phone lay beside him, screen black. He kept seeing Aleesya beneath the reservoir shelter, her face composed around a hurt she refused to let him soothe.

I did love you.

Maybe I still do.

But I’m not going to live inside maybe anymore.

The words had not been shouted. They had not needed to be. They moved through him with quiet violence, returning again and again whenever he tried to close his eyes.

He had thought confession would feel like release.

Instead, it had shown him the size of what he had damaged.

At two in the morning, he opened their chat.

His fingers hovered over the keyboard.

There were so many things he wanted to say now that he finally understood language was not the same as courage. I’m sorry. I was wrong. I love you. I should have said it sooner. Please don’t choose him. Please choose me. Please let me make this right.

Every sentence sounded like it wanted something from her.

So he put the phone down.

At three-fifteen, he picked it up again.

He opened the notes app instead.

The first line took almost ten minutes.

I am sorry for making you carry a question I was too afraid to answer.

He stared at it until his eyes burned.

Then he kept writing.

Not to send. Not yet. Maybe not ever.

But because Hafiz had been right.

If he spoke again, it had to be clean.

No vague care. No half-lit tenderness. No confession that was really a plea for comfort. If he wanted to apologize, he had to stop asking Aleesya to translate his fear into love.

He wrote until Subuh.

By then, his room had paled slightly at the edges. The window showed a blue-grey sky. Somewhere below, a motorcycle started. Water dripped from the awning outside in patient intervals, the same rhythm as every rain that had brought Aleesya into his life and every rain that had watched him fail to name her.

After prayer, Harith sat on the prayer mat with his hands open, unable to ask Allah for Aleesya to come back.

Not because he did not want it.

Because for the first time, he understood that wanting her was not the same as deserving ease.

So he asked for something harder.

“Ya Allah,” he whispered, voice rough in the quiet room. “Teach me not to harm what I love.”

The words left him small.

Honest.

And too late, perhaps.

Aleesya spent Sunday helping her mother reorganize the kitchen cabinets.

It was not planned. Her mother announced after breakfast that the container shelf had become “a national disaster,” which meant everyone in the house would eventually be recruited whether they volunteered or not. Her father disappeared too quickly to be innocent. Nadiah, who had come over with homework and the confidence of someone who did not live there, was caught within fifteen minutes and assigned the spice drawer.

There was comfort in domestic chaos.

Plastic containers emerged without lids. Lids appeared for containers no one remembered owning. Expired sauce packets were discovered behind a bag of flour. Nadiah found three identical bottles of vanilla essence and accused the household of hiding a bakery operation. Aleesya’s mother denied everything while moving two of the bottles into a different cabinet.

For a few hours, life became simple enough to hold.

“Why do we have so many biscuit tins?” Nadiah asked, holding up a red tin with gold flowers.

Her mother looked offended. “That one useful.”

“For what?”

“Many things.”

“It is empty.”

“Then it is ready.”

Aleesya laughed from where she sat on the kitchen floor surrounded by mismatched lids. The sound startled her with its ease.

Nadiah noticed. She always noticed the wrong things at the right time. Her gaze slid over Aleesya’s face, then down to her phone lying untouched near the rice cooker.

“So,” Nadiah said casually, too casually, “how was coffee yesterday?”

Aleesya reached for another lid. “Nice.”

“With Danish?”

Her mother stopped wiping a shelf.

Aleesya looked up. “Mama.”

Her mother resumed wiping with exaggerated innocence. “I’m cleaning. My ears are just nearby.”

Nadiah grinned. “Was he gentleman?”

“He was respectful.”

“Ooh. Adult word.”

“Respectful is not boring.”

“I didn’t say boring. I said adult.” Nadiah leaned closer, lowering her voice though their mother was absolutely listening. “And Harith?”

Aleesya’s hand paused on a container.

The kitchen fan hummed overhead. Outside, someone dragged a chair across a neighbouring flat, the sound faint through the wall.

“We talked,” she said.

Her mother’s wiping slowed again.

Nadiah’s grin disappeared. “Properly?”

Aleesya breathed in.

The smell of turmeric, dish soap, and old plastic filled the small kitchen. Ordinary smells. Safe ones. She focused on them for a second.

“More properly than before,” she said.

Nadiah sat back against the cabinet, hugging her knees. “And?”

“He said he wants me.”

Her mother finally stopped pretending.

Nadiah’s eyes widened. “He said like that?”

Aleesya nodded.

“Wah.” Nadiah blinked. “After Danish appeared?”

Aleesya gave her a tired look.

“Sorry,” Nadiah said, though she did not sound entirely sorry. “But timing matters.”

“I know.”

Her mother placed the cloth down carefully. “What did you say?”

“That I’m not choosing anything now.”

Her mother’s face softened with something like pride and sadness mixed together. “Good.”

Nadiah looked between them. “Good? But isn’t that what she wanted to hear?”

Aleesya smiled faintly. “It is.”

“Then why not good good?”

“Because people are not vending machines, Nad. You don’t insert confession and receive trust.”

Nadiah opened her mouth, then closed it. For once, youth paused before answering.

Aleesya looked down at the lids scattered around her. “I still love him. I think. But I also remember how small I felt when he called me his friend.”

The words sat in the kitchen quietly.

Her mother moved first. She lowered herself carefully onto the small stool near the sink, wiping her hands on a towel. “Love can be real and still not be safe to return to immediately.”

Aleesya nodded, throat tight.

Nadiah reached over and nudged a container lid toward her. It did not match anything. “For what it’s worth,” she said, softer now, “I’m proud of you.”

Aleesya looked at her cousin.

Nadiah shrugged, suddenly shy. “You know. For not just melting because he finally said what you wanted.”

Aleesya smiled, but it hurt.

“I almost did,” she admitted.

Her mother touched her shoulder.

That was enough. If she spoke more, she might cry in a kitchen full of biscuit tins, and somehow that indignity would be harder to survive than heartbreak itself.

So she picked up another lid and said, “This one confirm no container.”

Her mother immediately objected.

The world, mercifully, became containers again.

Harith sent the message on Monday evening.

He waited until after work, after Maghrib, after dinner with his family where he answered questions badly and ate less than enough. He waited until he was certain he was not sending it because loneliness had sharpened. He read the note he had written at three in the morning, then rewrote it into something smaller because Aleesya did not need an essay dropped into her lap like a burden.

Even then, his hand shook slightly before he pressed send.

> Harith
> Aleesya, I know I said a lot at the reservoir. I don’t want to add pressure. I just want to say this clearly once.
>
> I’m sorry for making you carry a question I was too afraid to answer. I’m sorry for calling us “just friends” after letting you give me more than friendship. I’m sorry for only becoming brave when I felt you leaving.
>
> You don’t have to reply. I’ll respect whatever space you need.

He placed the phone face down immediately after sending it.

Then he stood up, walked to the kitchen, poured a glass of water, drank it too quickly, and nearly choked.

His younger sister Hana looked up from the dining table, where she was studying with colourful pens arranged like weapons. “You okay?”

“Yes.”

“You look like you sent a risky text.”

Harith stared at her.

Hana’s eyes widened with delight. “Oh my gosh, you did.”

“Study.”

“You’re deflecting.”

“You’re failing math.”

“I’m not failing. I’m negotiating with numbers.” She capped a highlighter and leaned forward. “Is it Kak Aleesya?”

Harith said nothing.

Hana gasped as if he had confessed to a crime.

“I knew it.”

“You don’t know anything.”

“I know you get weird when her name is mentioned. Like your soul leaves your body but politely.”

Harith rubbed his forehead. “Why is everyone in this family dramatic?”

“We learned from Ibu.” Hana tilted her head, her teasing softening. “Did you mess up?”

Harith looked toward the living room, where his mother was watching television with the volume low, pretending not to listen the same way Aleesya’s mother probably pretended not to listen. He wondered if all mothers attended the same secret school.

“Yes,” he said.

Hana became very still. She was eighteen, but in that moment she looked older, or perhaps Harith was only realizing she had been growing up while he was busy being afraid of his own life.

“Did you say sorry?” she asked.

“I just did.”

“Properly?”

“I think so.”

She nodded with the solemn authority of someone who had never been in a serious relationship but had read enough online threads to form strong opinions. “Good. Now don’t make the apology a trap.”

Harith looked at her. “What?”

“You know. Don’t say sorry and then act sad if she doesn’t immediately make you feel better. That’s annoying.”

Harith stared at his sister.

Hana returned to highlighting. “Girls talk, Abang. We know things.”

He almost laughed.

Then his phone buzzed in his room.

He stopped breathing.

Hana looked up. “Go lah.”

Harith went.

The message was from Aleesya.

He sat on the edge of his bed before opening it.

> Aleesya
> Thank you for saying it clearly. I appreciate the apology.

That was all.

No warmth.

No opening.

But no cruelty.

Harith read it several times.

His chest hurt, but the hurt felt clean. Not relieved, exactly. Not hopeful enough to be dangerous. Just clean.

He typed:

> Harith
> Thank you for reading it.

He stopped.

Deleted the message.

No. That asked for a continuation.

He locked the phone and placed it down.

For once, he let her words be the end of the exchange.

Friday came with heat instead of rain.

By noon, the sky above Raffles Place was almost white, sunlight glaring off glass buildings and making the city feel sharper than usual. Aleesya walked to lunch with Mei Lin under the narrow strip of shade along the pavement, both of them squinting like people personally wronged by the weather.

“I hate this,” Mei Lin declared.

“You hate everything before food.”

“I’m consistent.”

They ended up at a crowded food court where the air smelled of fried noodles, soup stock, sambal, and too many people trying to eat within one lunch hour. They found seats near a pillar after circling twice. Mei Lin ordered ban mian. Aleesya bought chicken rice and barley.

She had been lighter since Harith’s apology.

Not healed. Not clear. But lighter in the way a room became easier to breathe in after someone finally opened a window, even if the mess remained inside. His message had not erased anything. It had not made her trust him again. But it had given shape to the wound. It told her she had not imagined the imbalance. It told her he understood at least enough not to ask her to soothe him.

That mattered.

It also made missing him more dangerous.

Because now, when she thought of him, she did not only think of the man who had hurt her. She thought of the man trying not to.

Mei Lin stirred chilli into her noodles. “You’re thinking again.”

Aleesya blinked. “How do you always know?”

“Your chewing becomes philosophical.”

Aleesya looked down at her rice.

Mei Lin leaned forward. “Update?”

“There’s no update.”

“That’s an update.”

Aleesya sighed, but there was no annoyance in it. “Harith apologized properly.”

Mei Lin’s brows lifted. “Define properly.”

Aleesya opened her phone and showed her the message.

Mei Lin read it silently, expression changing from skepticism to reluctant approval. “Okay. Not bad.”

“High praise.”

“From me, yes.” Mei Lin handed the phone back. “And how do you feel?”

“Tired.”

“That’s not a feeling. That’s your brand.”

Aleesya smiled faintly. “I feel… sad. Relieved. Angry. Soft. Annoyed that I’m soft.”

“Valid.”

“And Danish has been nice.”

“Also annoying?”

“Very. In a healthy way.”

Mei Lin nodded sagely. “The worst kind. Harder to complain about.”

Aleesya poked at her rice. “I told him I’m still sorting something out. He said coffee wasn’t a contract.”

Mei Lin placed a hand dramatically over her chest. “Green flag. Painfully green. Like traffic light asking you to proceed safely.”

“I don’t know if I want to proceed.”

“You don’t have to.” Mei Lin’s voice softened. “But maybe don’t decide based only on guilt. Not guilt toward Danish, not guilt toward Harith.”

Aleesya looked at her.

Mei Lin shrugged. “You can love someone and still need proof that they know how to love you properly. And you can appreciate someone good without owing them your heart because they arrived with manners.”

The food court noise seemed to dim for a moment.

Aleesya swallowed against the tightness in her throat. “When did you become so wise?”

“I’ve always been wise. You were distracted by my excellent sarcasm.”

Aleesya laughed.

Mei Lin smiled, then pointed her chopsticks at her. “Also, please eat. Emotional growth requires protein.”

Aleesya obeyed.

Danish noticed the change before Aleesya told him.

They met again the next Saturday, not for coffee this time but for a volunteer briefing at a small multipurpose hall near Kembangan. The organisation was preparing for a school supplies drive, and the room was full of exercise books, pencil cases, donated bags, and the faint smell of new stationery. Rain clouds gathered outside but had not yet broken. Ceiling fans turned lazily overhead, moving warm air from one corner to another without improving it much.

Danish found Aleesya near a table sorting notebooks by level.

“You look less like you’re holding your breath,” he said.

She glanced up, surprised.

He held up both hands. “Observation, not interrogation.”

Aleesya smiled despite herself. “Maybe I am.”

“Good.”

He picked up a stack of exercise books and began helping without being asked. For a while, they worked side by side, sorting Primary, Secondary, and blank notebooks into separate piles. Around them, volunteers chatted and taped labels onto boxes. Somewhere near the entrance, Cik Salmah complained that the marker ink was too faint and that nobody respected bold handwriting anymore.

Aleesya placed a stack of books into a carton. “He apologized.”

Danish did not ask who.

His hands paused only for a moment before continuing. “Properly?”

She smiled a little. Everyone seemed to use that word now. “Yes.”

“I’m glad.”

The answer was so simple that she looked at him.

He did not seem pleased, threatened, or wounded. Only sincere.

“You’re glad?”

Danish met her gaze. “You looked hurt when you mentioned him without mentioning him. If he apologized properly, then I’m glad for you.”

Aleesya did not know what to do with that kind of generosity.

She aligned the edges of a notebook too carefully. “Doesn’t that make things awkward for you?”

“A little,” he admitted.

The honesty made her look up again.

He smiled faintly. “I’m not a saint, Aleesya. I like you. Of course part of me would prefer if your heart had no unfinished business. But real people usually have histories. I don’t think pretending otherwise helps.”

She absorbed that quietly.

Outside, thunder muttered far away.

Danish placed another stack of books into the box. “I meant what I said before. Slow is fine. Unclear is not.”

The distinction settled between them.

Aleesya nodded. “That’s fair.”

“And if at any point you know your heart is elsewhere, I’d rather you tell me clearly than be kind vaguely.”

The phrase struck so close to the old wound that she had to breathe through it.

Kind vaguely.

Yes.

That was what Harith had been, for too long.

“I will,” she said.

Danish nodded once, trusting the answer enough not to ask for more.

At the doorway, someone entered carrying two cartons stacked dangerously high.

Harith.

Aleesya saw him at the same time Danish did.

Harith’s eyes moved from her to Danish, then back to the boxes in his arms. He greeted the coordinator and set the cartons down near the wall. His face was controlled, but not blank. There was a tiredness to him now, and a restraint Aleesya recognized from the reservoir—not the old retreat, but something more deliberate. He was careful not because he wanted to hide, but because he was trying not to take what was not offered.

Their eyes met briefly.

Harith gave a small nod.

Aleesya returned it.

No smile.

Not unkind.

Just honest.

Danish, beside her, picked up another stack of notebooks.

He did not comment.

That, too, was kindness.

The rain finally broke during the briefing.

It came down hard, turning the hall’s high windows into blurred sheets of water. The sound filled every pause, drumming against the awning outside, rushing through drains, softening the edges of people’s voices. Volunteers moved chairs closer to hear the coordinator explain delivery routes and beneficiary lists.

Aleesya sat between Cik Salmah and Danish.

Harith sat two rows behind.

She knew without looking.

The meeting stretched longer than expected because there was confusion over school levels, bag sizes, and whether the donated calculators needed batteries. Aleesya took notes. Danish asked practical questions. Harith clarified delivery timings once, his voice steady from behind her.

They became, for that hour, three people in the same room doing useful work.

It was almost peaceful.

Then the coordinator announced that two volunteers would need to stay back to check the final inventory because several boxes had been mislabeled.

Aleesya volunteered automatically.

Harith did too, one second later.

The room shifted.

Not obviously. But Danish glanced at Aleesya, then at Harith. Cik Salmah looked between them with elderly precision and decided, perhaps mercifully, to say nothing.

“I can stay instead,” Danish offered quietly.

Aleesya looked at him.

His face gave nothing away except the option itself.

She thought about accepting. It would be easier. Safer. Another way to avoid standing too close to the unfinished thing.

Then she looked toward Harith.

He was not watching her desperately. He was not silently asking her to choose him. He stood near the back with his hands clasped in front of him, waiting for her answer as if whatever she chose would be accepted.

That made her decision harder.

“No,” Aleesya said finally. “It’s okay. You have somewhere to be, right?”

Danish nodded slowly. “My mother’s. Dinner.”

“Go. I’ll manage.”

“All right.” He paused, then added gently, “Text me if you need anything.”

The phrase touched the old place, but lightly.

Aleesya nodded. “Thanks.”

As volunteers dispersed into the rain, Danish packed his bag and left after saying goodbye. He did not look back in a dramatic way. He did not try to communicate hidden disappointment through silence. He simply went, trusting her with her own choices.

Aleesya watched him disappear beyond the doorway, umbrella opening into the rain.

Then she turned back to the boxes.

Harith stood a few feet away.

For a moment, the hall felt too large around them.

“Where should we start?” he asked.

The question was practical.

She was grateful.

“Primary school boxes first,” she said, picking up the clipboard. “Some of the labels don’t match the inventory.”

They worked.

At first, they spoke only in numbers and item names. Ten pencil cases. Twelve rulers. Eight blue bags. Secondary textbooks in the wrong box. Calculators without batteries. Exercise books stacked by size. Rain hammered the roof so loudly that they had to stand closer than usual to hear each other, which felt like a test neither had studied for.

But work gave their hands somewhere to go.

Harith did not hover. He did not make unnecessary conversation. When Aleesya reached for a heavy carton, he said, “That one’s heavy,” then stopped himself from taking it. “Do you want help?”

The correction was small.

Aleesya noticed.

“Yes,” she said.

He lifted the carton onto the table, then stepped back.

“Thanks.”

He nodded.

They continued.

After an hour, the boxes had been relabeled. The rain softened but did not stop. The hall emptied fully, leaving only the two of them, stacked chairs, cartons, and the smell of wet concrete drifting in through the open doorway.

Aleesya checked the inventory one last time. “Done.”

Harith closed the marker cap. “Good.”

Silence returned.

Not empty. Not comfortable.

But different from before.

Harith placed the marker down carefully. “I won’t keep you long.”

Aleesya looked at him.

He stood across the table, rainlight dim behind him. His hair had grown slightly messy from the humidity. There was a faint ink mark on his thumb. He looked tired but clear-eyed.

“I just wanted to say,” he continued, “thank you for replying to my apology.”

“You don’t have to thank me.”

“I do.” His mouth moved into something almost like a smile, but it did not stay. “And I want you to know I’m not expecting anything from you because I said it.”

Aleesya held his gaze. “Okay.”

He nodded, accepting the smallness of her answer.

Then he looked down at the table, at the rows of labelled boxes. “I’ve been thinking a lot about what you said. About wanting the safety of being close without the responsibility of choosing.”

Aleesya’s fingers tightened around the clipboard.

Harith saw, but did not stop. His voice remained quiet. “You were right. I told myself I was protecting what we had. But really, I was protecting myself from having to risk anything. And I let you pay for that.”

The rain filled the pause.

Aleesya did not speak.

If she did, she might forgive him too quickly.

Harith drew a slow breath. “I don’t know if I can fix it. Maybe I can’t. But I don’t want to be someone who only becomes honest when he’s afraid of losing. So I’m trying to understand why I kept hiding.”

His eyes lifted to hers then.

“My parents’ marriage was not easy,” he said.

The sentence entered carefully, like someone stepping into a room that had been locked for years.

Aleesya stilled.

Harith had mentioned his parents before, but lightly. Ordinary things. His mother’s cooking. His father’s quietness. Family dinners. Hari Raya routines. He had never spoken of difficulty as something that shaped him.

He rubbed the ink mark on his thumb. It did not come off. “They’re okay now. Better. But when I was younger, there were years where everything felt like waiting for someone to say the wrong thing. My father wasn’t cruel. Not like that. But he shut down. My mother would ask what was wrong, and he would say nothing until nothing became the whole house.”

Aleesya’s throat tightened.

Outside, the rain slipped from heavy to steady, a softer rhythm against the roof.

“I think I learned early that naming things made them dangerous,” Harith said. “If you didn’t say you were angry, maybe the fight wouldn’t start. If you didn’t say you were scared, maybe no one could use it. If you didn’t say you loved someone too loudly, maybe you couldn’t fail them too obviously.”

He gave a small, humorless breath.

“I’m not saying this as an excuse.”

“I know,” Aleesya said quietly.

His gaze moved to her face, grateful and pained.

“I just want you to understand that when I called us friends, it wasn’t because you meant so little.” His voice roughened. “It was because you meant so much that I became a coward.”

Aleesya looked away.

There were some truths that hurt because they were cruel.

Others hurt because they arrived with tenderness after the damage had already learned its way around your heart.

Harith continued, softer now. “But fear doesn’t make it okay. I know that. I should have been honest before you had to protect yourself from me.”

The room blurred at the edges.

Aleesya blinked quickly, refusing tears.

A part of her wanted to cross the distance between them. To touch his ink-stained thumb. To say she understood, because she did. She understood fear. She understood families that taught love through silence. She understood not wanting to damage something precious by naming it wrong.

But understanding was not the same as returning.

She set the clipboard down.

“Thank you for telling me,” she said.

His face shifted—hope rising, then being restrained before it could ask too much.

Aleesya saw the effort.

That mattered too.

“I wish I knew what to say,” she admitted.

“You don’t have to say anything.”

The old Harith might have said the same sentence to sound noble while hoping she would comfort him anyway.

This Harith seemed to mean it.

Aleesya looked at him for a long moment. “I understand you better now.”

His throat moved.

“But I also understand myself better,” she said. “And I can’t go back to loving you quietly while you learn how to be clear.”

He closed his eyes briefly.

When he opened them, the pain was there, but so was acceptance.

“I know.”

“I’m not punishing you.”

“I know.”

“I’m still angry.”

“You should be.”

The answer broke something small inside her.

She almost laughed. Almost cried.

Instead, she said, “You’re making it difficult to stay angry.”

His mouth curved faintly, sadly. “Sorry.”

“That apology better not count.”

“It doesn’t.”

Silence settled again, but this time it did not cut.

Aleesya looked toward the doorway. Rain continued beyond the shelter, silver in the evening light. The world outside smelled washed and waiting.

“I should go,” she said.

“I’ll walk you to the bus stop if you want.” He paused, then added, “If not, I won’t.”

The choice lay cleanly in front of her.

No pressure.

No assumption.

Aleesya picked up her handbag.

“You can walk with me,” she said.

Harith nodded once.

He did not smile.

Maybe he knew better than to treat permission like forgiveness.

They shut off the hall lights and stepped into the corridor together.

The walk to the bus stop was familiar and not.

Rain fell beyond the covered walkway in steady threads, blurring the estate into grey-green softness. The ground was slick beneath their shoes. Water moved along the drains in fast, shallow streams. Somewhere nearby, the smell of frying onions drifted from a coffee shop, warm against the cool rain air.

Harith walked beside Aleesya without holding the umbrella over her, because they were under shelter and because perhaps he knew she did not need saving from weather that was not touching her.

For several minutes, they said nothing.

Their silence had changed again.

Not healed. Not easy.

But honest enough to breathe inside.

At the bus stop, they stood beneath the roof while rain dripped from the edge in shining beads. The arrival screen blinked: seven minutes for her bus.

Aleesya looked at the road.

Harith stood at a careful distance.

She thought of the many times he had stood too close and not close enough. The lift lobby. The prata shop. The wedding hall. The reservoir bench. All the places where they had almost become something and then retreated into safer words.

Her bus stop reflection in the glass showed them side by side but not touching.

A familiar ache rose.

This time, she did not run from it.

“Harith,” she said.

He turned immediately.

Not eager.

Present.

“If I had not pulled away,” she asked, “would you have ever said it?”

The question hurt him. She saw it. But he did not avoid it.

“I don’t know,” he said.

Honest.

Devastating.

Aleesya nodded slowly.

He swallowed. “I want to say yes. I really do. But the honest answer is I don’t know. Maybe I would have stayed scared because you were still there. Maybe I would have mistaken your patience for permission.”

She looked down at her hands.

There was the truth she had feared.

Not cruel.

Worse.

Real.

“Thank you,” she said.

His brows drew together. “For what?”

“For not making it prettier than it is.”

The bus lights appeared in the distance, pale through the rain.

Harith looked toward them, then back at her. “Aleesya.”

“Yes?”

He seemed to choose each word with care. “I love you.”

The sentence arrived differently this time.

Not like the reservoir confession, raw and urgent with jealousy still warm beneath it. This was quieter. Less desperate. More painful because it did not ask her to answer.

“I know that may not be enough,” he said. “I know it may be too late. But I want to say it without making you carry what comes after.”

Aleesya’s eyes burned.

For one second, the whole world seemed to narrow to rain, bus lights, and the man she had loved finally standing in the truth without pulling her toward it.

She wanted to say it back.

The words rose. They reached her throat.

But saying them would make something move, and she did not yet trust where it would go.

So she only nodded.

The bus arrived with a sigh of brakes.

The doors opened.

Aleesya stepped forward, then paused at the entrance. She turned back.

Harith stood under the shelter, rainlight on his face, hands at his sides. He looked ready to let her leave. Not because it did not hurt, but because he understood now that love without freedom was only another kind of taking.

That hurt most of all.

Aleesya held his gaze.

“Goodnight, Harith,” she said.

His smile was small and broken at the edges.

“Goodnight, Aleesya.”

She boarded.

As the bus pulled away, she did not look back immediately. She waited until the road curved and the shelter began to slip from view.

Then she turned.

Harith was still there.

Not following.

Not reaching.

Only standing in the rain-washed evening with the truth finally in his hands, too late to know whether it would be received.

Aleesya pressed her fingers lightly against the window.

The glass was cool.

Outside, the city blurred into silver lines, and for the first time since the wedding, she let herself cry.

Quietly.

Not because she had lost him.

Not because she had forgiven him.

But because somewhere between love and leaving, both of them had finally stopped pretending friendship was the name for what had broken.
