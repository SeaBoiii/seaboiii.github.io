Moving on did not look the way people promised it would.

It did not arrive with sunlight through curtains, a clean apartment, a new haircut, or a sudden appetite for all the things grief had ruined. It did not feel like a door opening. It did not feel like strength. Most days, it looked embarrassingly ordinary: replying to work emails, buying groceries, charging a phone, pretending not to remember dates, washing a mug before the tea stain became permanent.

Farid learned this slowly and without elegance.

Some mornings, he woke before his alarm and felt almost fine. The room would be grey with early light, Momo a warm weight against his shin because the cat had somehow learned to open his bedroom door if it was not properly latched. Farid would lie still and listen to Bunda moving in the kitchen, the radio murmuring low, a kettle beginning its small complaint. For a minute, sometimes two, his mind would not reach for Cindy.

Then it would.

Not dramatically. Not with the old violence of the first weeks. It would simply place her name in the room like a cup someone forgot to clear.

Cindy.

And there it was: the shape of absence at the edge of an otherwise normal morning.

Farid would sit up, rub both hands over his face, and begin the day anyway.

That, apparently, was moving on.

On Monday, he went to work early.

The office smelled of air-conditioning, coffee, and printer heat. People moved through the corridor with laptops tucked under arms, ID cards knocking softly against chests, half-finished conversations trailing behind them. Farid answered questions, joined meetings, fixed a report someone else had broken, and laughed when his colleague Raka complained about a client who sent “urgent” emails with no attachments and no context, which Farid agreed was a form of emotional violence.

At noon, he ate with Raka and Mei in the pantry.

Raka was describing his latest attempt at dieting while opening a packet of fried chicken.

“This is protein,” he said defensively.

Mei stared at him. “It is also regret.”

“Regret has macros.”

Farid laughed into his water.

The laugh surprised him by being real.

Raka pointed at him with a chicken wing. “See? Farid understands.”

“I understand nothing,” Farid said. “I’m just here.”

“That’s the most honest thing anyone has said in this office,” Mei said, stirring her instant noodles with a fork. “Put it on our company values.”

They continued like that for a while, complaining about work in the harmless way people complained when they were secretly grateful to be employed. The pantry window showed a narrow slice of city: glass towers, wet roofs, traffic compressed into red and white lines. Rain had not started yet, but the clouds had gathered thickly, promising their usual evening performance.

Farid listened more than he spoke.

He had become good at that.

When Raka asked about weekend plans, Farid said, “Dinner with Bunda. Maybe replace a sofa cover again if Momo wins.”

“Momo is the cat, right?” Mei asked.

“The landlord,” Farid corrected.

Raka leaned back. “You talk about this cat like he pays rent.”

“He damages property like he owns it.”

Mei smiled. “You should bring him to the office.”

“No. He’ll become management.”

They laughed. The conversation moved on.

For a few minutes, Farid felt himself inside the room instead of outside it looking in. He ate, drank, nodded, made jokes at the right rhythm. It felt like progress until he saw Mei tilt her phone toward Raka to show him a video of a cat attacking a sofa.

Orange cat. Grey fabric. Tiny claws.

Farid’s chest tightened with a tenderness so specific it almost embarrassed him.

Cindy would have sent that.

The thought arrived without permission, and with it came the familiar phantom movement of his thumb toward a chat that no longer opened into her life.

He placed his phone face down on the table.

Mei noticed. “You okay?”

“Yeah.” Farid reached for his water. “Just remembered I forgot something.”

“What?”

He smiled faintly. “To not let my cat watch training videos.”

They laughed again.

The moment passed.

The ache did not disappear. It simply became smaller, patient enough to sit quietly while Farid finished lunch.

Cindy discovered that wedding preparation had its own language, and none of it was designed for women who still flinched at certain kinds of tenderness.

Every conversation came with choices. Venue lighting. Flower types. Seating sections. Pre-wedding shoot locations. Menu tasting. Dress fittings. Invitation revisions. Whether to write “with joyful hearts” or “with gratitude to God” on the program card. Whether the reception should include childhood photos. Whether Adrian preferred a navy suit or charcoal.

The world seemed convinced that love became easier when converted into tasks.

Cindy tried to be grateful for that.

Tasks gave her hands something to do. Tasks gave her mother something to ask about besides whether Cindy was sleeping. Tasks gave Adrian ways to help without pressing too hard into the rooms she had not fully opened for him. Tasks turned the future into neat folders and checklists, which was useful because feelings did not obey columns.

On Tuesday evening, Cindy and Adrian attended a food tasting at the venue.

The ballroom was not too grand, which she liked. Warm lights hung low enough to soften the ceiling. The carpet had a pattern she did not hate. The stage was small, the aisle not too long, and the windows opened toward a garden that would probably look beautiful in photos if it did not rain.

It would probably rain.

Cindy had begun to believe rain followed unfinished things.

A coordinator led them to a round table covered in white cloth. Cindy’s parents were already there. Adrian’s parents arrived ten minutes later, polite and warm. Everyone greeted everyone else with the brightness of people determined to make a good memory.

Cindy sat beside Adrian with a folded napkin in her lap and smiled through soups, rice, roasted chicken, fish, vegetables, dessert cups, and her mother’s repeated concern that the sambal might be “too strong for older guests.”

Adrian leaned slightly toward Cindy when no one was looking.

“You like the second chicken better,” he murmured.

She looked at him. “How do you know?”

“You ate two bites before making your thinking face.”

“I have a thinking face?”

“Yes. It looks like you’re judging a small criminal.”

“That’s just my face.”

“No, your normal face is kinder.”

She tried not to smile. “Are you calling me judgmental?”

“Only toward chicken.”

The laugh that left her was quiet but genuine. Adrian smiled, then returned his attention to his mother, who was asking whether the dessert could be less sweet.

At the far side of the table, Cindy’s mother watched them with soft approval.

That approval should have warmed her without complication.

Instead, Cindy felt a strange sadness rise in her throat.

Everything here fit.

That was the ache.

No one looked worried about whether the ceremony would offend another family. No one spoke carefully around belief. No one asked the kind of questions that sounded practical but carried fear beneath them. Cindy’s mother looked relieved in a way she tried to hide. Her father joked easily with Adrian’s father. Adrian knew when to speak and when to let silence stay comfortable. Even the venue coordinator seemed satisfied, as if the paperwork of their lives aligned properly.

It fit.

Cindy took a sip of water.

For one dangerous moment, she imagined another table. Bunda beside Farid, hands folded around a glass, trying to be gracious while carrying more questions than anyone should bring to a wedding tasting. Farid sitting beside Cindy, quiet under the polite pressure of two families who liked each other but did not know how to build a bridge wide enough for forever. Cindy’s mother smiling too tightly. Her father asking something about the future in a tone that was not unkind, only afraid.

She imagined Farid’s hand near hers under the table, not touching, because they would both know touch could not solve the room.

Then she looked at Adrian beside her, listening carefully to her father discuss parking access.

Adrian belonged here.

Farid would have tried to belong until the effort broke something in him.

Cindy set down her glass.

Adrian noticed.

His hand moved under the table. Not to hold hers without warning. Just close enough that his knuckle brushed the side of her finger by accident or choice.

She let her hand remain.

After a moment, she turned her palm slightly and allowed their fingers to touch.

Adrian did not look at her.

He only continued listening to her father, his thumb resting lightly against hers beneath the white tablecloth, a small hidden kindness in a room full of public approval.

Cindy closed her eyes for one breath.

This, too, was real.

She had to remember that.

By Wednesday, Farid had begun running after work.

He did not like running.

That was partly why he chose it.

There was something useful about voluntary discomfort. It gave the body a reason for the tightness in the chest, the burning lungs, the strange pressure behind the eyes. Pain made more sense when it came from concrete. Distance. Sweat. Bad shoes. A hill. The body did not ask why the heart had not healed faster when both knees were complaining.

He started at the park near his apartment after Maghrib, when the evening air had cooled just enough to make effort possible. The jogging path curved around rain-dark trees and a small pond where the water reflected apartment lights in broken gold lines. Families moved slowly along the outer path. Couples shared earphones. Old men in sandals sat on benches discussing politics with the confidence of retired prophets.

Farid ran past them all with poor form and stubbornness.

By the third round, his side hurt.

By the fifth, he deeply regretted every life choice that had brought him here.

By the sixth, his mind became too busy negotiating with his lungs to think of Cindy.

That, he decided, was worth something.

Afterward, he sat on a bench, sweat cooling unpleasantly under his shirt, and watched dragonflies skim low over the pond. His breath came unevenly. His legs trembled faintly. Somewhere behind him, a child cried because her balloon had escaped and become a small red planet rising into the evening sky.

Farid drank from his water bottle.

His phone buzzed.

Bunda.

> **Bunda:**
> Are you running or pretending to run?
> Kamu lari atau pura-pura lari?

Farid smiled and took a photo of his shoes on the pavement.

> **Farid:**
> Evidence.

Bunda replied:

> **Bunda:**
> Shoes can stand still.
> Sepatu bisa diam.

He laughed, alone on the bench.

A woman nearby glanced at him and smiled politely. Farid nodded back, then looked at his phone again.

Another message appeared from Raka.

> **Raka:**
> Bro, we’re playing futsal Friday. Come. You need human interaction.

Farid typed:

> **Farid:**
> I interact with humans daily.

Raka replied:

> **Raka:**
> Work humans don’t count.

Farid looked at the pond.

A month ago, he would have declined automatically. Before that, when he was still with Cindy, Friday nights had often belonged to long calls, dinner with Bunda, or meeting Cindy somewhere halfway between tired and happy. After the breakup, Friday nights became dangerous. Too much empty time. Too much phone. Too much memory.

He typed:

> **Farid:**
> Okay. Count me in.

Raka sent seven shocked stickers and one message:

> **Raka:**
> HE LIVES.

Farid shook his head, smiling faintly.

The smile remained until he opened his gallery by accident while trying to close the chat.

A photo appeared.

Not Cindy’s face. Not even her hand. Just rain against a café window, taken two years ago. The caption in his gallery, automatically saved from her message, read:

> **Cindy:**
> Looks like your kind of weather. Dramatic tapi sok tenang.
> Looks like your kind of weather. Dramatic but pretending to be calm.

Farid’s thumb froze.

The park around him continued: footsteps on gravel, insects near the lamps, the distant call of someone selling drinks at the gate. His body still ached from running, but the older ache rose beneath it, less loud than before, more familiar.

He did not delete the photo.

He did not send it to himself.

He only closed the gallery and looked at the real evening water until the urge to reopen it passed.

That, too, was moving on.

Not forgetting.

Choosing not to reopen every door just because he still knew where the keys were.

Cindy’s first pre-wedding dress alteration took place on Thursday after work.

Melissa pinned the waist while Cindy stood on the platform and watched herself in the mirror. The gown was not the same satin one from the first appointment. This one had delicate sleeves and a simpler skirt. Her mother cried when she saw it, which had immediately made Cindy suspicious of both the dress and maternal tears. Adrian had not seen this one yet. Cindy wanted to surprise him.

Or maybe she wanted one decision to belong to herself before everyone else loved it.

Melissa crouched by the hem with a cushion of pins around her wrist.

“Breathe normally,” she said.

“I am.”

“You are breathing like a spy hiding evidence.”

Cindy exhaled.

Melissa smiled into the mirror. “Better.”

The gallery was quiet except for soft music and the faint rustle of fabric. Outside the tall windows, the sky had turned grey again. Afternoon rain threatened. Of course it did.

Cindy studied her reflection.

She looked beautiful.

The admission came softly, without vanity. The dress suited her. It did not erase her. It did not turn her into a decoration. The sleeves gave her comfort. The neckline was modest but graceful. The skirt moved when she shifted, not dramatically, just enough to remind her she had a body inside the ceremony everyone was planning around her.

She wanted to send a photo to someone.

Her mother already had seven.

Lia had demanded ten and sent back a voice note consisting mostly of screaming.

Adrian could not see it yet.

For half a second, the old instinct rose.

Rid would say something stupid first because he was nervous.

Not stupid. Gentle. Deflecting.

Maybe: “Is the dress safe from Momo?”
Maybe: “You look like someone who would reject my parking skills.”
Maybe nothing at first, because Farid sometimes went quiet when sincerity arrived too quickly.

Cindy’s eyes stung.

Melissa looked up immediately. “Too tight?”

Cindy shook her head. “No. It’s fine.”

Melissa did not believe her but returned to the pins. Professionals in bridal galleries probably learned when not to ask.

Cindy gripped the sides of the skirt lightly.

“I think this is the one,” she said.

Melissa’s face brightened. “Really?”

Cindy looked at herself again.

Not because the dress solved anything.

Because it did not have to.

“Yes,” she said. “This one.”

After the fitting, she changed back into her work clothes and sat alone in the small lounge while Melissa processed the alteration notes. The rain had started, tapping against the windows with polite fingers. Cindy watched the drops slide down the glass, separating and rejoining like undecided roads.

Her phone buzzed.

A message from Lia.

> **Lia:**
> I am still emotionally recovering from the dress. You looked illegal.

Cindy smiled and replied:

> **Cindy:**
> That sentence makes no sense.

> **Lia:**
> Beauty often doesn’t.

Another message appeared below it, this one from Adrian.

> **Adrian:**
> How was the fitting?

Cindy typed:

> **Cindy:**
> Good. I chose one.

His reply came after a pause.

> **Adrian:**
> I can’t wait to see you in it.

A simple message.

No pressure. No demand for a photo. No “send me.” No teasing accusation. Just anticipation, respectful and warm.

Cindy rested the phone against her chest.

She whispered, “He really does treat me well.”

The room did not answer.

But saying it aloud felt important.

As if rehearsing for a future conversation she did not yet know she would have.

Friday night futsal was a disaster.

Farid lasted twelve minutes before Raka announced that he had the stamina of a haunted Victorian child.

“You invited me,” Farid said, bent over with both hands on his knees.

“Yes, for friendship, not competitive advantage.”

Mei, who had joined because she played better than half the men and enjoyed proving it, jogged past them and said, “Stop flirting and defend.”

“I am dying,” Farid said.

“Die near the goal then.”

The indoor court smelled of rubber, sweat, and the sharp sweetness of isotonic drinks. Shoes squeaked. Someone shouted for a pass. Someone else cursed dramatically after missing an open shot. Farid’s shirt clung to his back. His lungs burned. His legs had begun sending formal complaints.

And yet, when Raka passed him the ball near the end of the game and Farid accidentally scored because the goalkeeper slipped, everyone cheered as if he had won an international tournament.

Raka tackled him into a hug.

“He returns!”

“It was an accident.”

“All greatness is an accident.”

Mei pointed at him. “Do not get arrogant. It was ugly.”

“It counted,” Farid said, smiling.

The game ended with everyone sweaty, laughing, and accusing everyone else of cheating. They gathered outside the court afterward beneath a metal awning while rain fell hard enough to blur the parking lot lights. Someone passed around bottled water. Someone ordered late-night nasi goreng from a nearby stall. Raka complained about his ankle while standing perfectly fine.

Farid sat on the curb, exhausted in a way that felt clean.

For once, his body had used up enough of him that his thoughts were quiet.

Raka dropped beside him. “Good you came.”

Farid drank water. “You just wanted someone worse than you.”

“True. But also good you came.”

Farid looked at him.

Raka was not usually careful. His care arrived disguised as insults, invitations, and sending memes at inappropriate hours. But tonight, beneath the court lights and rain noise, his expression held something honest.

Farid capped his bottle. “Thanks.”

Raka nudged his shoulder. “Come next week too.”

“We’ll see.”

“That means no.”

“That means we’ll see.”

“It means no in polite Farid language.”

Farid smiled. “Maybe.”

“Maybe means maybe no.”

“You’re exhausting.”

“I’m friendship.”

Farid laughed.

A message buzzed in his pocket.

For the first time in a long time, he did not feel immediate dread or hope.

It was Bunda.

> **Bunda:**
> If you are still alive, buy eggs on the way home.
> Kalau masih hidup, beli telur balik nanti.

Farid replied:

> **Farid:**
> Barely alive. Will buy.

Then, after a moment, he sent a second message:

> **Farid:**
> I scored.

Bunda’s reply came quickly.

> **Bunda:**
> Accidentally?

Farid stared at the phone.

Raka leaned over, read it, and burst out laughing so loudly two people turned around.

Farid shook his head.

“She knows you,” Raka said.

“Unfortunately.”

But warmth spread through him, small and steady.

On the way home, he bought eggs and, without thinking, a packet of chamomile tea.

He noticed only at the cashier.

The box was already on the counter beside the eggs.

The cashier scanned it.

Farid said nothing.

At home, Bunda unpacked the bag and lifted the tea with one eyebrow.

“You drink this now?”

Farid took off his shoes. “Maybe.”

“You said it tastes like flower water pretending to be medicine.”

He froze.

Bunda looked at him. They both knew whose phrase had returned to the kitchen.

Farid took the box gently from her hand.

“Maybe medicine is useful sometimes,” he said.

Bunda said nothing.

Later, at half past one in the morning, after showering and eating reheated rice, Farid made a cup.

It tasted exactly as bad as he remembered.

He drank it anyway.

Cindy did not use the anonymous chat app that week.

She considered this progress.

Or avoidance.

The difference depended on the hour.

Instead, she filled her evenings with useful things. She met Mama to discuss flowers. She called Lia about bridesmaid dresses. She reviewed the program flow. She helped Adrian choose between two suit colors and pretended not to be amused when he took the decision more seriously than expected.

“Navy says dependable,” he said over video call, holding two fabric swatches near his shoulder. “Charcoal says mature.”

Cindy sat at her desk with her chin in one hand. “You are both dependable and mature.”

“That doesn’t help.”

“Navy looks better with warm lighting.”

He lowered the charcoal immediately. “Done.”

“That was fast.”

“You’re the lighting expert.”

“I’m not.”

“You are now.”

She smiled.

Adrian looked at her through the screen. He was in his room, still wearing his work shirt, hair slightly messy. The warm lamp behind him made him look softer than usual.

“You look tired,” he said.

“I am tired.”

“Wedding tired or life tired?”

Cindy considered. “Both.”

“Want to talk about it?”

She looked away from the screen.

The question was generous. Too generous for a night when her thoughts had already been circling the shape of an orange cat and a scratched sofa.

“Not tonight,” she said.

Adrian nodded. “Okay.”

No disappointment. No wounded silence. No forced cheer.

Just okay.

Cindy looked back at him. “Does that ever frustrate you?”

“What?”

“When I say not tonight.”

Adrian leaned back slightly. “Sometimes I worry. That’s different.”

“Only worry?”

He gave a small smile. “I’m human. Sometimes I wish I knew how to help faster. But I don’t think forcing you open counts as helping.”

Her throat tightened.

“You’re very reasonable,” she said.

“I hide my dramatic side well.”

“I don’t believe you have one.”

“I once wrote a very angry product review for a kettle.”

Cindy laughed. “What did the kettle do?”

“Leaked on my table and betrayed my trust.”

“That is dramatic.”

“Thank you.”

The conversation lasted another twenty minutes, gentle and unimportant in the way good conversations could be when two people were trying to build a life. After they hung up, Cindy sat in the soft silence of her apartment and felt gratitude settle beside grief like two cups on the same table.

Her phone screen dimmed.

She did not unlock it again.

Not immediately.

At 1:48 a.m., she did.

No messages.

No missed calls.

No reason to be awake.

She opened her browser and stared at the last closed tab.

Omegle.

She did not click.

The blue-white glow of her phone lit her face in the dark. Outside, rain whispered against the balcony glass. The apartment was still, the wedding folder sleeping in the drawer, the dress chosen, the guest list sent, the future moving forward with or without her full permission.

Cindy placed the phone face down.

Then turned it face up again.

“Rid would say this is pathetic,” she murmured.

No. That was not true.

Farid would not say pathetic.

He would tilt his head, tired and gentle, and say, “That is so not you.”

Her chest tightened.

She locked the phone.

At two in the morning, she was still awake.

Farid was also awake.

The chamomile had failed as medicine but succeeded as memory.

He sat at his desk in the dark, laptop open, a half-finished work document glowing on the screen. He had told himself he was only checking one file before bed. That had been at 12:40. Now the clock showed 2:03, and the document had not changed in thirty minutes.

Momo slept on the sofa behind him, one paw hanging over the scratched cover. Bunda’s room was quiet. Rain tapped lightly against the window, not heavy enough to drown thought, only steady enough to accompany it.

Farid rubbed his eyes.

He should sleep.

Tomorrow was Saturday. No early meeting. No urgent deadline. No reason to remain at the desk except the old reluctance to enter bed with a mind still awake.

He opened a new browser tab.

For a while, he did nothing.

The blank search bar waited.

Farid typed half a word. Deleted it. Typed another. Deleted that too.

He had no desire to check Cindy’s profile. That was progress. He had no intention of messaging her. That was restraint. But there remained a restless need in him, not for her exactly, not even for comfort. For noise. For a stranger’s voice, maybe. For proof that the world contained people who did not know the shape of his loss and therefore could not step carefully around him.

He remembered Cindy once calling random video chat sites digital haunted houses.

He smiled faintly.

She had been right.

Then, because the night had weakened his judgment and the tea had done nothing except taste like an apology, Farid typed a URL he had not visited since university.

The page loaded.

Simple. Ugly. Familiar in a way that belonged to younger, more foolish versions of people.

A button waited.

Start.

Farid stared at it.

“This is stupid,” he said under his breath.

Momo did not wake to disagree.

He closed the tab.

The room returned to his work document.

Farid lasted twelve seconds.

Then he opened the tab again.

Across the city, Cindy stood in her kitchen drinking water directly from the glass because Adrian’s message had guilted her into hydration hours after the fact.

The water tasted faintly metallic from the filter.

Her laptop sat open on the desk where she had abandoned it after the video call. The room was dark except for the screen’s dim glow and the city beyond the window, blurred by rain. She should have gone to bed. She should have been proud of herself for not opening the site all week. She should have believed that progress meant choosing sleep before loneliness became curious.

Instead, she carried the glass to the desk and sat down.

The browser history waited like a bad idea.

Omegle.

Cindy clicked before she could talk herself out of it.

The page appeared, blunt and unpretty.

She looked at her reflection in the dark corner of the screen. Hair loose over one shoulder. Oversized shirt. Tired eyes. No bridal glow. No careful future. Just a woman awake at two in the morning, wanting to be no one for a few minutes.

“Just one,” she whispered.

She clicked Start.

A stranger appeared and vanished immediately.

Cindy exhaled.

Next.

A man wearing headphones said, “Where are you from?”

She panicked and clicked Next.

A black screen.

Next.

A girl painting her nails.

Next.

A ceiling.

Next.

Cindy leaned back, almost amused despite herself.

It was stupid.

It was harmless.

It was not harmless at all, but not in the ways people warned about.

The danger was not strangers. The danger was how easy it felt to become one.

Farid’s first stranger was a boy eating instant noodles too close to the camera.

“Bro, you look sad,” the boy said.

Farid blinked.

“Hello to you too.”

The boy slurped noodles. “Am I wrong?”

Farid considered.

“Yes,” he lied.

The boy nodded, unconvinced. “Okay, sad bro.”

Farid clicked Next.

He laughed after the screen changed.

The second stranger was a blank wall.

Next.

The third was a man playing guitar badly but with confidence. Farid listened for thirty seconds, admired the courage if not the tuning, then clicked Next when the man began singing a song Farid did not recognize.

The fourth stranger was a woman in a dorm room who waved and asked him his favorite movie. Farid almost answered honestly, then realized he had no emotional energy to explain why his favorite movie was tied to an old date with Cindy where the cinema air-conditioning had been too cold and she had fallen asleep halfway through.

Next.

The screen reset.

Farid leaned back.

Rain touched the window. Momo snored. The clock moved to 2:17.

He should stop.

Instead, he pressed Next again.

Cindy’s next stranger was an older man in a dim room who disconnected the moment she appeared.

Good, she thought.

Next.

Two teenagers sharing a chair shouted, “Hi!” so loudly Cindy flinched and laughed at the same time.

“Hi,” she said.

“Are you married?” one of them asked.

Cindy froze.

The other teenager smacked his arm. “You can’t ask that first!”

Cindy clicked Next before they could recover.

Her heart beat faster than necessary.

On the next screen, there was only darkness and the sound of someone breathing.

She clicked Next immediately.

Then came a woman in a kitchen smoking near a window.

Cindy stilled.

For one strange second, she wondered if it was the same woman from the other night. Same weary eyes, maybe. Same blunt sadness. But this woman looked up, squinted, and said something in Spanish before disconnecting.

Cindy let out a breath.

The apartment felt warmer now.

She drank more water.

“Last one,” she told herself.

She clicked Next.

The screen went black for a moment.

Loading.

Farid glanced toward the sofa.

Momo had shifted in his sleep, rolling onto his back, belly exposed, one claw caught in the fabric. Farid shook his head.

“Shameless,” he whispered.

The next stranger failed to load.

The screen spun.

Farid looked at the time again.

2:21.

He imagined Bunda waking for water and finding him talking to strangers online like a man in a cautionary tale. She would not be angry. Worse, she would be disappointed in a specific maternal way that made him feel twelve years old.

He moved the cursor toward disconnect.

Then the video connected.

For half a second, the image was blurred.

A dim room.

A woman’s outline.

Long dark hair.

Blue light.

Farid’s hand went still on the trackpad.

The picture sharpened.

And the stranger on his screen had Cindy’s face.
